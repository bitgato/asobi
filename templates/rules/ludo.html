Rules

Players Two to Four

Description

The players race their four pieces from start to finish according to the rolls of a single die.

Special areas of the Ludo board are typically coloured bright yellow, green, red, and blue. Each player is assigned a colour and has four pieces in their colour.

The board is normally square with a cross-shaped playspace, with each arm of the cross having three columns of squares, usually six per column. The middle columns usually have five squares coloured; these represent a player's home column. A sixth coloured square not on the home column is a player's starting square.

At the centre of the board is a large finishing square, composed of coloured triangles atop the players' home columns (thus depicting arrows pointing to the finish).

Gameplay

This one might be a little different from what you're used to because of custom house rules.

The game creator gets the first turn to roll the dice. Until a player gets a six to free one of his pieces from his yard, the turns rotate. When a player does roll a six, he gets a turn to roll again. Now, this rolled number is the actual value by which he can move his freed piece.

If a player has one or more pieces in play and rolls a six, he gets an additional roll and then gets the choice to either move one of his freed pieces by 6 + new roll or free a new piece and move it by new roll. If he rolls a six again, he gets another roll and then has the choice to either move one of his freed pieces by 12 + new roll or free a new piece and move it by 6 + new roll. If he rolls a six a third time, he may not roll the dice again or move a piece and the turn immediately passes to the next player.

Once players have one or more pieces in play, they select a piece and it is moved forward along the track by the number of squares indicated by the die. If an opponent's piece is blocking the path, the piece stops at the square and the opponent's piece is returned to its owner's yard.

There are some safe squares on the board where no pieces get returned to the yard. These include the piece owner's starting point (A red piece is safe only on the red starting point. It can still get captured and returned to the yard if it's on another player's starting point) and home column. Additionally, there are four squares marked with a ★ where all pieces, irrespective of the owner, are safe.

A player's home column squares are always safe, since no opponent may enter them. In the home column, a player cannot jump over; after one rotation is completed, the player must enter the home and roll the exact number needed to get each piece onto the home triangle.

Passes are not allowed. If no move is possible, the turn passes to the next player.

Example

Green player rolls six for the first time.

Green player gets another turn and rolls a four this time.

Green pieces that can be freed or can move are indicated as shown.

Green player selects the first piece and the piece is moved four squares after freeing.

The game continues until one of the players has all the pieces at home. If there are more than two players, the game continues until all players, except one, have all of their pieces at home.
